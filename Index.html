Key milestones from both the EU and Chinese regulatory frameworks, converging toward comprehensive governance of AI systems.

Directive 95/46/EC — first harmonised EU data protection framework, predating AI.

European Commission's first risk-based framework for AI regulation.

World's first comprehensive AI regulation; introduces GPAI-specific obligations including systemic risk threshold (10²⁵ FLOPs).

Personal Information Protection Law — China's "GDPR", but without a legitimate interest basis or independent supervisory authority.

Interim Measure on Generative AI — world's first GenAI-specific regulation, focused on content safety and consent.
2024

Model AI Law draft — comprehensive AI law with a de-identification safe harbour for foundational model training.

KEY STRUCTURAL DIFFERENCE
PIPL lacks a legitimate interest provision entirely. For GPAI systems, this means only consent is available — but consent is practically unattainable at web-scraping scale. MAIL's de-identification safe harbour attempts to bridge this gap.
EU AI Act

AIA Risk Classification & GPAI Obligations

The AI Act's risk-based pyramid, plus a dedicated GPAI layer — including the systemic risk threshold that triggers heightened obligations.

CLICK EACH TIER TO EXPAND
I
Prohibited / Unacceptable Risk
Outright banned AI practices
Real-time remote biometric surveillance in public spaces for law enforcement; social scoring by public authorities; subliminal manipulation below conscious perception; exploitation of vulnerabilities (age, disability); predictive policing based solely on profiling.
II
High Risk
Conformity assessment + registration required
Critical infrastructure (water, energy, finance); education (exam assessment, admissions); employment (hiring, performance monitoring); essential services (credit, insurance); law enforcement; migration & asylum management; administration of justice. Requires data governance, documentation, human oversight, accuracy measures.
III
Limited Risk
Transparency obligations only
Chatbots and conversational AI must disclose they are AI. Deep synthesis systems (deepfakes) must label synthetic content. General transparency so users can make informed decisions about interaction.
IV
Minimal / No Risk
No mandatory obligations
Spam filters, AI-powered recommendation systems, AI in video games, simple automation. Encouraged to voluntarily adopt codes of conduct, but no mandatory requirements under AIA.
GPAI
General Purpose AI Layer
Cross-cutting obligations for all GPAI providers
All GPAI: Technical documentation, compliance with EU copyright law (TDM exceptions), summaries of training data content, publish open-source models can have reduced obligations.

Systemic Risk GPAI (>10²⁵ FLOPs training compute): Mandatory adversarial testing ("red teaming"), incident reporting to the Commission and national authorities, cybersecurity protection for model weights, algorithms, servers and datasets, protection against unauthorised access and model leakage.
AIA REGULATORY SANDBOX
Controlled testing environments
AIA Article 57–63 allows providers to evaluate AI systems in real-world scenarios before full deployment, processing personal data collected for other purposes under strict conditions.

Limitation: The sandbox scope is narrow — most GPAI models would be excluded. Sandbox processing is time-limited and subject to GDPR compliance even during testing.
INVERSION ATTACKS UNDER AIA
Common obligation: Technical robustness against adversarial inputs, including model inversion attacks that could reconstruct training data.
High-risk AI: Specific dataset governance requirements to prevent personal data extraction.
Systemic risk GPAI: Explicit cybersecurity policies, physical and cyber access controls, and mandatory protections for training datasets — directly addressing inversion attack threat.

SIX STRUCTURAL DIVERGENCES (Chapter 6)
§ 6.1.4
No fundamental right to data protection
China's PIPL derives from internet security law, not constitutional rights. No equivalent to EU Charter Article 8.
§ 6.2.1
No independent supervisory authority
The Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) is a government body; EU Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) are constitutionally independent.
§ 6.1.2
No judicial review of constitutionality
Chinese courts apply law but cannot review its constitutionality. No CJEU equivalent exists. The NPC Standing Committee holds sole interpretive authority.
§ 6.1.3
Party control of legal system
Despite reform rhetoric, the CCP retains control over judicial appointments and legal policy. "Rule of law" without constitutional separation of powers.
§ 6.2.2
Sectoral regulatory origin
PIPL evolved from sector-specific network security law, not from privacy as an inherent right. This shapes its AI-era interpretation and enforcement posture.
§ 6.4.2
State security priority
National security and social stability override individual rights when they conflict. China's AI governance (IMGAI, MAIL) consistently privileges content safety and societal order over individual autonomy.
Conclusion

Key Findings

The thesis concludes that the fundamental rights framing in EU law is not merely symbolic — it produces measurably different outcomes for GPAI governance.

01
GDPR + AIA
Legitimate interest is the only viable but contested basis
For GPAI training on web-scraped data, Article 6(1)(f) legitimate interest is the most feasible legal basis, but requires a three-step balancing test and remains contested by commentators who argue no client relationship exists between AI providers and data subjects.
02
AI ACT
AIA complements but does not replace GDPR for data protection
AIA's GPAI documentation, adversarial testing, and cybersecurity requirements supplement GDPR without displacing it. The regulatory sandbox is too narrow to benefit most GPAI providers.
03
PIPL
China's consent-only approach creates a practical gap
Without a legitimate interest basis, PIPL forces reliance on consent for GPAI training — practically impossible at the scale of web scraping. MAIL's de-identification safe harbour attempts a pragmatic workaround, but remains draft-stage and untested.
04
IMGAI / MAIL
China's AI rules prioritise content safety over individual rights
IMGAI and MAIL focus on illegal content, national security, and social stability. Safeguards against model inversion attacks exist but are framed as cybersecurity rather than individual privacy protection.
05
Comparative Analysis
Textual similarity conceals structural inequality
PIPL mirrors GDPR's vocabulary but lacks the constitutional basis, independent enforcement, and balancing mechanisms that make EU data protection effective. Without a fundamental rights framing, the system remains politically subordinate.
06
Shared Challenge
The "black box" problem is unsolved in both systems
Both EU and Chinese frameworks struggle with AI transparency: neither GDPR's Art.13–14 disclosure requirements nor PIPL's Art.17 can practically trace individual data subjects hidden in massive web-scraped training datasets.
07
Normative Conclusion
Fundamental rights framing is not merely symbolic
The EU's anchoring of data protection in Article 8 of the EU Charter creates a floor that cannot be traded away for AI innovation. China's approach demonstrates that without this foundation, AI-era data rules tend toward surveillance enablement rather than individual protection.
